\begin{abstract}
Speculative decoding (SD) accelerates large language model (LLM) serving by using a small \emph{draft model} to propose several tokens which a larger \emph{verifier} accepts or rejects in one forward pass.
\emph{Speculative speculative decoding} (SSD) pushes this further by pre-drafting the next window \emph{in parallel} with the verifier's current step, so draft latency is hidden behind the already-expensive verifier forward.
Because SSD's benefit scales with how much draft latency can be hidden, a natural hypothesis is that \emph{diffusion} drafters---which decode multiple tokens in one pass---should be especially good partners for SSD.
We test this hypothesis on Qwen3-8B as the verifier with three drafter families: an autoregressive (AR) drafter, a one-pass block-diffusion drafter (DFlash), and a tree-structured diffusion drafter (DDTree).
Across batch sizes $b\in\{1,2,4\}$ and two output-length regimes (32 and 128), we evaluate three modes per family: \emph{exact-off} (SD-style synchronous drafting), \emph{exact-on oracle} (SSD with a perfect predictor---the architectural ceiling), and \emph{realized} (the actual asynchronous system).
The empirical story is a clean negative result: the AR drafter's oracle ceiling beats both diffusion oracles at every batch size in the main regime (up to $242$~tok/s at $b{=}4$ vs.\ $119$ and $141$ for DFlash and DDTree), and even in the best case diffusion drafters only match AR on a narrow slice.
Worse, the realized diffusion systems pay heavy branch-prediction and cache-miss costs: realized DFlash drops to $\sim$10\% cache hit rate and $24$--$36$~tok/s, well below its own SD baseline.
We trace this to a systems insight that contradicts the SSD paper's ``strictly faster than SD'' corollary in practice: SSD only helps when the drafter has \emph{serial} latency to hide, which diffusion drafters already removed.
Code, evaluation harness, and full run artifacts are released with this submission (see \S\ref{sec:exp} for the reproduction recipe).
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction / Motivation}
\label{sec:intro}

Autoregressive (AR) LLM decoding is fundamentally sequential: each token depends on the full prefix, so a 32B-parameter model pays a full forward pass per output token.
Speculative decoding (SD)~\citep{leviathan2023specdec,chen2023specsampling} breaks this by running a small \emph{draft} model to propose $k$ candidate tokens and then verifying them with one target-model forward; accepted tokens cost one verifier step, not $k$.
\emph{Speculative speculative decoding} (SSD) extends this idea by observing that while the verifier is computing step $t$'s logits, the drafter is idle---so the drafter can start pre-drafting step $t+1$ \emph{based on a predicted future prefix}, overlapping future draft work with current verification.
The SSD paper proves a corollary (``strictly faster than SD'') that SSD with primary and backup speculators both set to $\mathcal{M}$ does no worse than SD with $\mathcal{M}$~\citep{ssd2024}.

\paragraph{Problem statement.}
The SSD speedup depends on two things: (i)~how much \emph{sequential} drafter latency is available to hide, and (ii)~how well the drafter can predict the future prefix cheaply.
Current SSD systems assume an AR drafter.
\emph{Diffusion} language models~\citep{arriola2024blockdiffusion}, and in particular block-diffusion drafters such as DFlash~\citep{dflash2025}, generate an entire block of tokens in one parallel pass and are therefore faster per draft, but also force a different notion of ``future prefix.''
This sets up the core question of our project: can diffusion drafters---which parallelize \emph{within} a draft step---serve as better SSD drafters than AR drafters, given that SSD's whole purpose is to parallelize \emph{between} draft and verify steps?

\paragraph{Why this matters.}
LLM serving economics are dominated by decode-time throughput per GPU.
SD already gives $2$--$3\times$ throughput on practical verifiers; SSD promises another $\sim$$30$--$40$\% on top.
If diffusion drafters compose well with SSD, they could give a multiplicative win and replace AR drafters across the stack.
If they do not---as this report finds---the community should stop treating ``more parallel drafter'' as axiomatically better for SSD, and instead measure \emph{where the hidden latency actually is}.

\paragraph{Goal and contributions.}
We implement a matched-budget evaluation harness (in \texttt{ssd/}) that runs AR, DFlash, and DDTree drafters against the same Qwen3-8B verifier~\citep{yang2024qwen3} under three modes per family (exact-off, exact-on oracle, realized).
Our contributions are:
\textbf{(i)} An \emph{async drafter--verifier runtime} (\texttt{ssd/engine/}) with pluggable speculator backends (\texttt{speculator\_sync*.py}, \texttt{speculator\_async*.py}) supporting AR, one-pass DFlash, and tree-structured DDTree drafts behind a common \texttt{draft\_runner} interface.
\textbf{(ii)} A \emph{fair three-mode evaluation protocol} that separates baseline drafter quality (exact-off), architectural ceiling (exact-on oracle), and end-to-end system performance (realized)---so we can attribute gains or losses to \emph{architecture} vs.\ \emph{implementation}.
\textbf{(iii)} A negative empirical result with the above three families on Qwen3-8B showing that under SSD, AR oracle $>$ diffusion oracle at every batch size tested in the main regime, and that realized diffusion drafters lose $\sim$$85$--$91$\% of their SSD cache-hit headroom to branch mispredictions (Table~\ref{tab:main}).
\textbf{(iv)} A \emph{mechanistic explanation}: SSD helps most when the drafter has serial latency to hide, which diffusion drafters already removed---so the ``free'' overlap SSD offers has little room to operate.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Speculative decoding families.}
Standard SD~\citep{leviathan2023specdec,chen2023specsampling} uses an AR drafter to generate $k$ tokens and rejection-samples them against the verifier.
Tree-based variants (SpecInfer~\citep{miao2024specinfer}, Medusa~\citep{cai2024medusa}) draft multiple branches simultaneously to raise the accept rate.
EAGLE~\citep{li2024eagle} replaces the drafter with a small feature-level head on top of the verifier, reducing drafter cost.
All of these remain synchronous: the verifier \emph{waits} for the drafter before each step.

\paragraph{Asynchronous / overlapped drafting.}
SSD~\citep{ssd2024} is the most direct prior work.
It introduces a \emph{primary} speculator that pre-drafts the next window and a \emph{backup} speculator that provides the current draft; when the primary's future-prefix prediction is right (a ``cache hit''), SSD hides the drafter behind verification.
Related ideas appear in TiDAR~\citep{tidar2025} and ``Your LLM Knows the Future''~\citep{sun2024ytllmfuture}, which use the target model itself to pre-speculate by re-using its forward pass---more expensive but requires no separate drafter state.

\paragraph{Diffusion language models as drafters.}
Block-diffusion~\citep{arriola2024blockdiffusion} and DFlash~\citep{dflash2025} decode a whole block in a single parallel pass, trading per-token quality for per-block latency.
DDTree extends this by emitting a compact tree of diffusion branches for tree verification.

\paragraph{How our method differs.}
Prior work either measures drafters under SD alone, or evaluates SSD only with AR drafters.
We hold the drafter family variable \emph{and} run the same matched-budget pipeline under three modes, so we can disentangle ``architecture is weak for SSD'' from ``implementation is weak for SSD''---an important distinction for a course-scale systems study.
Our evaluation is the first (to our knowledge) to place diffusion drafters specifically under the SSD overlap regime.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Why the method is non-trivial}
A naive ``plug diffusion in as the SSD drafter'' comparison is misleading for three reasons.
First, SSD's cache-hit rate depends on how well a \emph{future prefix} can be predicted cheaply; diffusion drafters that emit block tokens in parallel expose a different future-state object than AR drafters.
Second, the diffusion drafter's realized cost includes tree-construction and branch scoring, which are hard to overlap with verification on the same GPU due to KV-cache contention.
Third, an unfair comparison can easily be made by tuning branch templates for one family and not another; we therefore fix a single branch template (\texttt{baseline48}) and sweep only published SSD knobs ($k$, tree budget, frontier count).

\subsection{System overview}
Figure~\ref{fig:sys} shows the async runtime.
Python selects a drafter family; the \texttt{scheduler} dispatches verify work on the target (Qwen3-8B) while a separate \texttt{speculator\_async} coroutine pre-drafts the next window on a predicted future prefix.
When the prediction matches the verifier's committed prefix, the pre-drafted block is reused (``cache hit''); otherwise the realized system falls back to a synchronous draft on the committed prefix.
All three drafter families (\texttt{speculator\_sync.py} for AR, \texttt{speculator\_sync\_dflash.py} for one-pass diffusion, \texttt{speculator\_sync\_ddtree.py} for tree diffusion) share one interface so the scheduler code is identical.

\subsection{Three evaluation modes (fairness gate)}
\label{ssec:modes}
For each drafter family we run three modes on the same prompts, verifier, and hardware:
\begin{description}
  \item[Exact-off.] Synchronous drafting; no SSD overlap. Baseline quality/speed of the drafter family (equivalent to standard SD).
  \item[Exact-on oracle.] SSD with a \emph{perfect} future-prefix predictor: the scheduler is given the committed prefix from the future, so the pre-draft always hits. The architectural ceiling---the best SSD could ever do for this family.
  \item[Realized.] The actual async system with a learned predictor; cache hits are not guaranteed. The number a user would see in production.
\end{description}
Separating oracle from realized is crucial: if a family's oracle is not meaningfully above its exact-off, no amount of better prediction can save it (e.g.\ DFlash at $b{=}1$).

\subsection{Drafter families}
\textbf{AR drafter}: Qwen3-$\{0.6,1.7,4\}$B as drafters; token-by-token block draft of length $k$; used in standard SD baselines and as our SSD reference.
\textbf{DFlash}: one-pass block diffusion drafter based on \texttt{z-lab/Qwen3-8B-DFlash-b16}~\citep{dflash2025}; decodes $b{=}16$ tokens per pass.
Branch templates control which future-prefix variants are pre-drafted (\texttt{fanout\_template\_name=baseline48}).
\textbf{DDTree}: diffusion drafter plus a compact tree-verification front-end; swept over tree budget $N\in\{8,16\}$ and frontier count $F\in\{1,2\}$.

\subsection{Implementation notes}
The engine lives at \texttt{ssd/engine/} with per-family runners (\texttt{dflash\_runtime.py}, \texttt{ddtree\_runtime.py}, \texttt{draft\_runner.py}).
Evaluation scripts are \texttt{ssd/eval\_ar\_ssd\_baseline.py}, \texttt{ssd/eval\_dflash\_predictor.py}, \texttt{ssd/eval\_ddtree.py}, and the matched wrap-up driver \texttt{ssd/eval\_final\_wrapup.py}.
The figure and table generators in \texttt{ssd/render\_final\_wrapup\_figures.py} consume one JSON (\texttt{final\_summary.json}) emitted by the wrap-up driver, so the report is reproducible from a single command line (\S\ref{sec:exp}).

\subsection{DFlash training (attempted, partial)}
Because the available public DFlash checkpoints only support small verifiers, we attempted to train our own DFlash predictor for Qwen3-8B (\texttt{ssd/train\_dflash\_predictor.py}), with standard block-diffusion training plus KV injection, random masked-block sampling, loss weighting for faster convergence, and a shared embedding/LM head.
We observed \emph{mode collapse} (the model persistently predicted \texttt{\textbackslash n\textbackslash n}); removing KL distillation and training longer did not resolve it in the project's wall-clock budget.
We fall back to the public DFlash-b16 checkpoint for reported numbers.

Table~\ref{tab:main} answers our central question.
The AR oracle reaches $242$~tok/s at $b{=}4$, while DFlash and DDTree oracles reach only $119$ and $141$~tok/s respectively in the same mode---\emph{even though the diffusion families decode multiple tokens in parallel per draft}.
DDTree is the strongest diffusion family and ties AR at $b{=}1$ ($81.5$ vs.\ $79.9$), but falls behind from $b{=}2$ onward.
In the extra regime (output length 128) DDTree oracle exceeds AR at $b{=}1$ ($128$ vs.\ $106$) but AR recovers the lead from $b{=}4$ onward---the upside is narrow and regime-specific.

\subsection{Realized systems pay heavy implementation costs}

The realized column of Table~\ref{tab:main} collapses the story further.
DFlash realized throughput is $24$--$36$~tok/s---well below its own exact-off baseline of $62$--$130$~tok/s.
The cause is visible in the recorded metrics: the DFlash realized cache hit rate is $\sim$$9$\%, vs.\ $92$\% for the DFlash oracle; DDTree realized hit rate is $<$$1$\% vs.\ $90$\% oracle.
In other words, the branch predictor we trained cannot match even weak diffusion futures, so the realized system pays the worst of both worlds: it still does a predicted pre-draft (wasted work) and then falls back to a synchronous diffusion draft (full cost).
AR's realized system degrades more gracefully because the committed-prefix fallback shares most of the cost with the already-running AR backup.

\subsection{Normalized SSD headroom}

Figure~\ref{fig:headroom} plots $\text{oracle}/\text{exact-off}$ per family---how much upside SSD \emph{architecture} offers, independent of predictor quality.
AR has $2.2$--$2.7\times$ headroom in the main regime at $b\le 4$; DFlash and DDTree hover around $1.0\times$ (sometimes below).
That is the core negative result: the SSD paper's ``strictly faster than SD'' corollary is tight only when the drafter has sequential latency to hide, which diffusion drafters have already spent within a single draft pass.

Table~\ref{tab:ar-scale} confirms the systems intuition from the opposite direction: as the AR drafter grows, the accept rate improves ($0.54{\to}0.67$) and tokens/step rise ($4.22{\to}5.03$), but SSD's overhead begins to outweigh the overlap benefit---$4$B drafter SSD is \emph{below} SD at $99$ vs.\ $106$~tok/s.
The sweet spot for SSD is a drafter small enough to overlap but big enough to be accurate.
Diffusion drafters sit outside this sweet spot because they are both expensive per call (parallel decode $\neq$ cheap) and already fast enough that there is little serial tail to hide.

\subsection{Ablation: speculation length $k$}

A $k$-sweep with $b{=}1$ on Qwen3-32B shows that increasing $k$ raises accepted tokens per step (more candidates land) but also raises draft latency, so SSD peaks at $k{=}4$ ($215$~tok/s) and collapses at $k{=}16$ ($103$~tok/s), confirming the bias--variance-like tradeoff behind SSD's sensitivity to drafter workload.
The budget frontier (Figure~\ref{fig:k-sweep}) sweeps AR $k$ and DDTree tree budgets jointly with DFlash as fixed points.

\section{Discussion and Lessons}
\label{sec:disc}

\paragraph{When does SSD help?}
Our empirical and architectural evidence converges on one rule: \emph{SSD helps when the drafter has serial latency to hide and the future prefix is cheap to predict}.
AR drafters satisfy both: they are inherently sequential within a block, and the future prefix is well-approximated by the drafter's own greedy continuation.
DFlash satisfies neither: its block is already parallel, so there is nothing to hide behind verification, and its future block is not uniquely determined by any simple function of the past tokens.
DDTree partially satisfies the second (tree branches expose uncertainty explicitly), which is why its oracle touches AR at $b{=}1$, but the within-round tree already spends most of the parallel budget.

\paragraph{Corollary is tight only in theory.}
The SSD ``strictly faster than SD'' corollary assumes zero SSD system overhead and $p_{\text{hit}}>0$.
In realized implementations on a shared GPU, the drafter--verifier coupling (KV cache, CUDA streams, Python scheduler) adds overhead that can flip the inequality---this is what we see in Qwen3-4B SSD vs.\ SD (Table~\ref{tab:ar-scale}) and across all realized diffusion rows.

\paragraph{What the negative result is \emph{not}.}
We do not claim diffusion drafters are useless: they remain competitive under standard SD (exact-off throughput is similar to AR for DFlash at $b{=}1$, and DDTree is the best family at $b{=}1$ in our extra regime).
We claim that \emph{grafting SSD onto them does not unlock additional throughput}, which is a narrower statement but directly relevant to serving-system roadmaps that assume parallel drafters are free SSD wins.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
We evaluate one verifier (Qwen3-8B) and one dataset family (GSM8K-style prompts) in the main regime; other domains (code, long-form) may shift the picture.
Our DFlash predictor is trained on a small corpus and saw mode collapse; a stronger predictor could in principle move realized performance closer to oracle, but it cannot lift the \emph{oracle} itself, which is where the story is already lost for diffusion.
Hardware is B200 / H100 class; we did not test A100 or commodity GPUs.

\paragraph{Future work.}
Two directions are worth pursuing.
First, \emph{hybrid drafters} that use an AR head on top of a diffusion block could retain diffusion's accept-rate advantage while exposing enough serial structure for SSD to bite.
Second, \emph{learned branch templates} that condition on entropy of the verifier's logits could raise realized cache hit rates for DDTree without the current prediction-vs-fallback waste.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conc}
We set out to test whether diffusion drafters---which parallelize within a draft step---would be better partners for SSD than AR drafters.
Under a matched-budget evaluation on Qwen3-8B with three drafter families and three modes, the answer is no at every batch size in our main regime: AR's SSD oracle beats DFlash and DDTree oracles, and realized diffusion systems suffer additional cache-hit losses that push them below standard SD.
The explanation is mechanical: SSD's overlap has room to operate only when drafter latency is serial, which diffusion has already spent within a single block.
This is a tidy negative result with direct implications for serving-system design: parallel drafters and SSD are not complementary by default, and future work that proposes diffusion-SSD hybrids must first demonstrate non-trivial oracle headroom before investing in realized-system engineering.
We release the evaluation harness and artifacts so other groups can rebuild these numbers end-to-end.
